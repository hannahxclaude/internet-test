HOMES NOT HOTELS

Protect Our Neighborhoods

Gates Pass • Tucson, Arizona

You used to know your neighbors. Now you watch strangers roll suitcases up the driveway every weekend.

Across Tucson, the house next door is increasingly likely to be a vacation rental — not a home. Gates Pass and neighborhoods throughout Pima County are feeling the squeeze as out-of-state investors snap up houses and convert them into commercial operations.

Our ask is simple: respect property rights, restore local control, and let communities decide what happens in their own neighborhoods — without interference from Phoenix or Washington.

What's Happening Here

6,000+ short-term rentals in Tucson alone — 90% are single-family homes

Your Neighbor's House Is Now a Business

Out-of-state investors buy up homes and convert them to vacation rentals. The profits leave Arizona. The problems stay, and you pay for it.

Families Can't Compete

Tucson rents rose 45% between 2019-2024, while wages grew only 27%. Every home turned into a vacation rental makes it harder for working families to stay.

The Revolving Door

New guests every few days. Cars you don't recognize. Noise at hours when neighbors should be sleeping. This isn't how a neighborhood is supposed to be.

Phoenix Overreach

In 2016, powerful special interest groups lobbied the state to strip local communities of the power to make their own decisions about short-term rentals. That's government overreach — and communities across Arizona are fighting to restore local control.

"Companies will move in and buy up available housing stock, convert them into short-term rentals. That money doesn't stay here. It also drives down the supply of affordable housing." — Supervisor Jennifer Allen, Pima County District 3

What We're Asking For

Common-Sense Local Solutions

Accountability, transparency, and the freedom for communities to govern themselves — that Pima County can implement now:

  • Create a permit/license system for STR operators (with the maximum allowable fee of $250). This provides a pathway for suspending permits for bad actors and generates revenue for enforcement.
  • Enable tax compliance tracking — Permitting data allows the Assessor's office to identify STRs and confirm they're paying Arizona's Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT) — essentially a sales tax on lodging that many operators currently avoid.
  • Establish an online registry/dashboard so neighbors and HOAs can easily identify STRs in their area and understand their rights.
  • Advocate for local control through county resolutions and lobbying the state legislature to restore regulatory authority to local governments.

Sample Message to County

Subject: Support Short-Term Rental Accountability in Pima County

Dear Supervisor,

As a resident of Pima County, I am writing to urge action on short-term rental accountability. Our residential neighborhoods are being transformed by commercial vacation rental operations, and we need the county to take meaningful steps within its existing authority.

I respectfully request that Pima County:

1. Establish a permit/license system for STR operators with the maximum allowable fee ($250). This creates accountability and provides a mechanism to suspend permits for bad actors.

2. Use permit data to ensure tax compliance. The Assessor's office should be able to identify STRs and confirm they are collecting and remitting Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT) — Arizona's lodging tax that many operators currently avoid, shifting the burden to compliant taxpayers.

3. Create a public online registry so neighbors and HOAs can easily identify STRs in their communities and understand their rights.

4. Advocate for restoring local control through official county resolutions. Communities should have the freedom to govern themselves without mandates from Phoenix.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

Sample Message to State Reps

Subject: Restore Local Control Over Short-Term Rentals

I'm writing about SB 1350, the 2016 law that stripped Arizona cities and counties of the power to regulate short-term rentals in their own communities.

This is a local control issue. I believe communities — not Phoenix, not Washington, and certainly not out-of-state corporations — should decide what's appropriate in their own neighborhoods. The 2016 law was a gift to Airbnb and Expedia, pushed through with bipartisan support after heavy lobbying. It was government overreach then, and it's government overreach now.

The results speak for themselves:

• Tucson alone has 6,000+ short-term rentals — 90% are single-family homes removed from the housing market
• Rents have skyrocketed while wages haven't kept pace and houses sit empty
• Residential neighborhoods have become commercial districts without any say from the people who live there
• Out-of-state investors extract profits while communities bear the costs

I'm asking you to:

1. Support legislation that restores the right of cities and counties to regulate short-term rentals as they see fit
2. Oppose any further preemption of local authority on this issue
3. Stand with Arizona homeowners and communities over corporate special interests

This isn't a partisan issue — it's about whether Arizonans have the freedom to govern their own communities. I hope you'll stand with your constituents and against out-of-state special interests.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]

Know Your Rights

  • HOAs may have CC&Rs that restrict or prohibit short-term rentals — check your community's governing documents
  • STR operators are required to collect and remit Arizona Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT)
  • Nuisance complaints (noise, parking, etc.) can be reported to Pima County code enforcement
  • Document issues with photos, dates, and times to support any formal complaints
