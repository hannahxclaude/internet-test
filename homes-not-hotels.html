HOMES NOT HOTELS

Protect Our Neighborhoods

Gates Pass • Tucson, Arizona

Short-term rentals are transforming our residential neighborhoods into commercial districts. Houses that once held families now operate as de facto hotels, straining infrastructure, disrupting communities, and eroding the character of places like Gates Pass.

Arizona state law currently limits what counties and cities can do to regulate STRs. But there are still meaningful actions our county can take right now — and we can advocate for restoring local control.

The Problem

Housing Shortage

Every home converted to a short-term rental is one fewer home available for families, workers, and long-term residents.

Neighborhood Disruption

Rotating strangers, late-night noise, parking overflow, and a revolving door of guests erode the fabric of residential communities.

Tax Avoidance

Many STR operators fail to collect or remit Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT), shifting the tax burden to compliant residents and businesses.

No Accountability

Without a permit system, there's no way to track STRs, identify bad actors, or enforce existing regulations.

What We're Asking For

County Action Under Existing Law

Even under current state constraints, Pima County can take meaningful steps:

  • Create a permit/license system for STR operators (with the maximum allowable fee of $250). This provides a pathway for suspending permits for bad actors and generates revenue for enforcement.
  • Enable TPT compliance tracking — Permitting data allows the Assessor's office to identify STRs and confirm they're paying required taxes.
  • Establish an online registry/dashboard so neighbors and HOAs can easily identify STRs in their area and understand their rights.
  • Advocate for local control through county resolutions and lobbying the state legislature to restore regulatory authority to local governments.

Sample Message

Subject: Support Short-Term Rental Regulation in Pima County

Dear [Representative/Supervisor],

As a resident of the Gates Pass area in Tucson, I am writing to urge action on short-term rental regulation. Our residential neighborhood is being transformed by the proliferation of STRs operating as de facto hotels, and we need the county to take meaningful steps within its existing authority.

I respectfully request that Pima County:

1. Establish a permit/license system for STR operators with the maximum allowable fee ($250). This would create accountability by providing a mechanism to suspend permits for bad actors, while generating revenue for enforcement activities.

2. Use permit data to ensure tax compliance. The Assessor's office should be able to identify STRs and confirm they are collecting and remitting Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT). Currently, many operators avoid these obligations, shifting the burden to compliant taxpayers.

3. Create a public online registry/dashboard so neighbors and HOAs can easily identify STRs in their communities and understand their rights under existing law.

4. Advocate for restoring local control through official county resolutions and lobbying the state legislature. Communities should have the authority to regulate commercial activity in residential areas.

Homes should be for neighbors, not tourists. I urge you to take aggressive action within existing constraints while working toward meaningful reform at the state level.

Thank you for your attention to this matter.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Your Address]

Know Your Rights

  • HOAs may have CC&Rs that restrict or prohibit short-term rentals — check your community's governing documents
  • STR operators are required to collect and remit Arizona Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT)
  • Nuisance complaints (noise, parking, etc.) can be reported to Pima County code enforcement
  • Document issues with photos, dates, and times to support any formal complaints
